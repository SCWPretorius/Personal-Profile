The title of this article might sound controversial in today’s world, where there is an increasing emphasis on work-life balance. Many companies and individuals are advocating for boundaries, with the idea that weekends and personal time should be spent relaxing, recharging, and doing non-work-related activities. But what if I told you that coding over the weekend could actually be part of a healthy work-life balance—especially for those of us who are passionate about technology?

Before you raise an eyebrow, let me clarify: I’m not suggesting that anyone should be pressured into working outside their regular hours or that burnout is a badge of honour. Far from it. Instead, I’m speaking to the software engineers, the tinkerers, and the technologists who find joy in building side projects, exploring new technologies, or solving complex problems in their free time.

For people like us, coding is more than just a job—it’s a craft, a creative outlet, and a form of self-expression. Just as an artist might spend their weekend painting or a writer drafting stories, a programmer might feel compelled to write code. In fact, dedicating time to personal projects can enhance creativity, improve technical skills, and provide a sense of accomplishment that’s entirely separate from the pressures of a 9-to-5 job.

Many of the most successful developers I know have been deeply involved in side projects throughout their careers. These side projects offer the freedom to experiment with new languages, frameworks, or hardware without the constraints or timelines that come with work-related tasks. Not only do these projects feed our curiosity, but they also help us stay up-to-date with emerging trends in technology—something that’s essential in a rapidly evolving field like software development.

What’s more, the skills developed during these personal projects often translate directly into our professional lives. Whether it’s discovering a more efficient algorithm, learning how to better optimize code, or mastering a new technology, the knowledge gained can make you a better developer at work. For instance, I’ve often found myself drawing on things I’ve learned from my weekend coding sessions in my professional projects, giving me an edge when facing technical challenges.

The key here is balance. Writing code over the weekend shouldn’t feel like an obligation or something that eats into your personal time. It should be something that excites you—an opportunity to explore new ideas, develop your craft, and grow as a developer. There’s a big difference between coding because you have to and coding because you want to. 

The reality is that work-life balance isn’t a one-size-fits-all concept. For some, balance means completely unplugging from technology on weekends, while for others, it means working on side projects that inspire them. It’s about finding what works best for you. If writing code on the weekend gives you joy and a sense of fulfilment, that’s just as valid as spending time on any other hobby.

So, did you write code over the weekend? If the answer is yes, celebrate that. You’re nurturing your creativity, growing your skills, and feeding your curiosity—all things that will not only make you a better developer but a more fulfilled one. After all, the best code often comes from those moments of unpressured inspiration, when we’re free to experiment, explore, and create without deadlines hanging over us.
